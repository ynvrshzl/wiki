1: the function of the human being.
so this section of the philosophy/book, focuses on introducing the groundwork, in order to ellaborate on the first book/section. 

understanding what the human being is, at the base, functional, physical, tangible, biological (etc.) level. 

the most significant point in this section is this idea: the human being is more sensitive to deprivation, than abundance.

emphasis: (a) how did i discover this, (b) why does this matter, (c) what does this mean about the human condtion (d) what is practically applicable.

(a): i discovered this (and continue to) through various methods:

- extensive research, observation
- minimal private journaling and building a public wiki on these topics
- (the most close-to-source method) my own lived experience 
- what i am capable of recording in these observations. 

i noticed that i the longer i spend living a "normal, healthy life" i almost become "numb" to abundance. it is the classic quote "yo do not know what you have until you lose it." that is the foundational definition of what it means to be human i believe.

our senses are somewhat logarithmic in scale, but arguably, the human sensory system isn't necessarily "processing" simuli; it is selectively choosing what to ignore.

so it's somewhat of an exclusion engine. we feel lack more than we feel supply. and this fundamental part of our biology, is essentially the human condition, as far as i can understand.

 my biology begins to treat the mundane as background static noise.

however, any period of serious deprivation, (wether it was sleep deprivation, food deprivation (dangerous) and especially social deprivation...) restores the human sensory system, and for a brief period of time, something akin to a spiritual understanding of depth/hypersensitivity: "oh my God, this is precious beyond what i can ever comprehend" to otherwise completely mundane everyday parts of life. such as sleep, food, socializing. the exact same domains of deprivation.

infact, the opposite is true aswell; we ignore what is abundant, to some degree.

so a certain level of chase is always required to maintain the equillibrium; and the fundamental gap between deprivation and abundance, is where the human being, as an individual, and a collective social net, is most sane.

this branches into two topics: sanity, and the equillibrium. which themselves, are the contination of the above points (b) and (c).

sanity is scary to converse about, becusae it's one of the core functions of human health, and also extremely sensitive to breaking. the equillibrium is supposed to act as an exoskeletal backbone, so-to-speak, to protect the self.

lastly, our point earlier (d) what is practically applicable from knowing "you don't know what you have until you lose it."

this is the part i'm still actively researching. it's a miracle we can even understand the beginning of this work at all, given how many health issues i have been battling. speaking of which, that brings me to the next section:

section 2: the human being is beyond physical.

what does this mean. not spiritual necessarily, but this idea: we can overcome and survive beyond what the human body was designed for. we can heal. we can replace. to a certain degree, we can survive alot more than we will ever know... perhaps that is for the best.

this is why throughout the entire body of work, i refer to the human being as "a creature of contradiction, that somehow coexists between chaos and order." throughout the entire work, the symbol is the "yin-yang" to express not only contrast between extremes, but equillibrium... etc.

it is single handedly a symbol that has saved my life, and i mean that in every weight of the word, "my life." i would  not exist if it weren't for this ancient chinese philosophy, wrapped in a beautiful infinite symbol. I hope to contribute in much the same way with my human life.

section 3: the human being is an organic constraint machine (change title)

fundamentally, it may seem cruel in some perspectives, to realize most authors, musicians, poets, painters, visionaries, polymaths, neurodivergents (etc) will most likely never get to share their brilliant work with us.

the author for example, is the archetype i'm most intimate with, since i see myself as a polymath author, studying the self. i realize this fundamental tragedy, might be our greatest "feature": most of the author's work will never be seen. not becuase it didn't survive the first draft, but becuase, it never made it to paper to begin with.

the internal imagination vs. the ability to output vs. the ability to edit output into shareable work, is the main tension force that drives the creative archetype.

now, my theory is that this not only applies to creative types: but to the human condition in general.

if our abilities are so constrained...
- biological hardware (anatomy, one mouth, one mind, two eyes, ten fingers, etc.) but 
- technological-like software (brain, petabytes of storage, neural networks, thousands of thoughts per day)

this means there are only two logical gates at this stage of the human condition:
- either this is just unfair
- or it's our greatest constraint

ellaboration: in jorge louis' litereature of the infinite library, we come to a devastating, yet clear conclusion: infinite knowledge, is not infinite power or infinite autonomy.

coming back to the first section: 
- the human sensory system, itself only works becuase it is selectively excluding information; not merely focusing. our senses are a careful processing of "what matters right now" and "what could matter in the near distance."
- negative space is what gives contrast to objects in paintings. 
- music is a careful balance of space between notes. (as famously quoted by debussy)

countless examples of constraint being the fundamental mechanic which makes experiencing anything possible.

section 4: consciousness and autonomy

this movement will attempt to explore the most important parts of the human self: consciousness and autonomy. the duality of our nature. what we think, vs what is real. what we imagine vs what is tangible. what we know vs what we don't know.

beyond the philosophy, we look at the tangible observation that i've discovered: the lens of consciousness.

what this means is that our consciousness, our beliefs, our perspective, shapes our perception, and radically, fundamentally, changes how we experience life.

this is massively important and a central topic that orbits closely to the heart of this philosophy/study.

becuase it means we have the power to do very little; which has enourmous cascading effects throughout our system.

"is the glass half empty, or half full?" may be a classic, somewhat, cliche understanding of our perspective shifting abilities, but as applied to more difficult situations, the human being essentially is equippied with a magnification lens, in which we can choose to "flip" or "shift" our perspective. this is not only visual, it is fundamentally cognitive. thus "the lens of consciousness" is a fundamental philosophical-scientific object i'm studying.

even more practically: existentialism vs locality. looking at the stars, and the imcomprehensible magnitude of the observable universe, can fundamentally worsen or cause 
- cynicism: "life is unfair and most people don't want to help others."
- existentialism: "we are too insignificant to be worthwhile"
- nihilism: (very dangerous) "nothing matters."
- solipsism. this one is (extremely dangerous.) i mean it. some people are sensitive, like myself. and if there is ever a set of dangerous, harmful beliefs, nihilism and sollipsism are absolutely destructive. 

part of the motivation behind this entire project was realizing "wait, nobody has warned me about dangerous beliefs... and here i am..... questioning wether my existence is worth the pain..."

the lens of consciousness is not only philosphical/scientific magnification and perspective... but our ability to choose. autonomy. 

this, as far as i have scaveneged the far depths of humankind's philosophy, history, and my own lived experience, is my understanding of the problem presented by nihilism; "does the suffering of being human, outweigh the positive parts of this life?"

our autonomy is the ability to choose. our consciousness presents two functions of change (there are plenty more, which we are still observing and recording) but beyond that, i have discovered this: yes. it is not only worth enduring suffering, but some human beings cannot live meaningfully, otherwise.

what do i mean by this? doctors. surgeons. first-responders. hotline recievers. dispatchers. firefighters. swat. millitary. nurses.

these people fundamentally keep our world from collapsing. we barely acknowledge they exist. most war veterans never recieve a "soldier salute" outside of their service. 

to a nihilist, that may seem like "yeah, they are just blind, life is pain." and half of that is true; life indeed contains pain, unsoveable issues, and cruelty beyond comprehension. and yet, the other half is healing, renewal, strength, fortitude, virtue, ethics.

that's the dark side of the moon, soto speak, that the dark philosophies never acknowledge. they only see life from one side "pain. evil. injustice." but they fail to capture the full object "sacrifice, duty, pursuit, victory."

this branches into two fundamental topics: the yin-yang, and the lens of consciousness, when processing both sides; becomes a force of uncomfortable truth of duality: without contrast there is no experience.

it does not mean "evil makes happiness possible" that is pragmatically accurate (contrast creates experience), but semantically biased and non-falsifiable. what is more accurate and testeable is how our own sensory system works. without contrast, there is no distinction between object, background, foreground etc. contrast is what creates boundary, and shapes our perception, both physically and philosophically.
