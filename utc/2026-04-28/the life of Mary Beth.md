Mary was born a beautiful woman. she, along with her twin, little sister, elisabeth, we're born from two wealthy parents. they were part of the royal mooder family. 

Mary thought that because she was beautiful, she could get away with anything, just as she saw other beautiful girls get their way in the city. eventually, she finds herself at the hands of a mafia. (this is where her story explores extreme boundaries of human trauma and really grotesque part of human life and nature. but it illustrates how someone becomes evil. and that is important to understand, to avoid becoming) 

Mary eventually realizes, her beauty is not a gift, it was something she was born with outside of her control. she did not choose to be beautiful. if she would have chosen, she would have never been born beautiful. this is where niallism starts to infest her thoughts. we also explore a radical paradox, with human being constantly wrestles with things they did not choose. but the human being also wrestles with, "the Grass is always Green on the other side " syndrome. 

some people wish they were born beautiful. other people wish they were born ugly. 

eventually Mary has to outgrow her shell of the beautiful innocent girl, and she becomes a monster. not by choice but by necessity. is survival.she becomes a deranged psychopath who sees human life as a fundamental tragedy. she does not actively work to erase existence, which is a deeply important philosophical distinguishing element of her story. her evil cannot be ambiguous; fundamentally protects though she loves, and destroys whatever comes near them. 

this is illustrated by a key moment in her life story, where she tries to escape the mafia that kidnapped her. she realizes she stands no match for a man. he taunts her. breaking Mary psychologically. until what is left is essentially her undying wrath, helplessness, hopelessness, and the rage of being a woman, being beautiful, living in a world fundamentally run by men. 

Mary Beth is an exploration on psychopathy. "monsters are not born, they are created." that's both the key philosophical perspective of her life story, and her favorite quote. it encapsulates who she is as a character, as persona, as an essence. if Joey Doom captures the suffering of humanity and the undying Spirit to survive, Mary Beth explores the expression of the undying spirit, that refuses to die, and is willing to survive at any cost. 

there are key moments in her life story where this becomes paradoxical, her humanity does not want to survive, she wants to die. she wants to put an end to this misery this madness. but something somewhere inside her continues forward.  unstoppable, like an undying specter of wrath. 

this is encapsulated by Mary Beth's spirit weapon, her cards. she uses cards to slice, but cards are also logical. her cards however, are rigged. symbolizing how she sees life. "we are born in a world where we cannot choose what we begin with, only what we end up with. the cards given to us are never fair."

also emphasizes the importance of helping those who are weaker than herself. "though the cards I was given, are unfair, we still have a choice. I choose to be there for my people. our brothers and sisters."
