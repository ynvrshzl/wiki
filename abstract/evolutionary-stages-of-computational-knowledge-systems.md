# Evolutionary stages of Computational Knowledge Systems 

Understanding the structural evolution of how problems exist, and solutions emerge, reveals the purpose of mechanics in a knowledge system. this is essentially a information overload problem at its most fundamental model, it is chaos management. signal over noise. 

So in essence, this is a study of complexity/chaos/noise management. and how we human beings deal with complexity in everyday life, and what tools and models we can use from separate domains, to make information and navigable and practically useful.

## 2.0 Titles (Chapters, Sections, Etc.)

A title is a compression of data. (e.g. a label for stuff, hidden inside a box)

Chapters, sections, scenes and more, typically data models that are found in literature. however they express the next proceeding evolutionary structure, which is the title abstraction. in essence a chapter contains a number and usually a title. that title itself is the structure because it compresses the content into a label.

## 3.0 Links
When a file becomes too large, the information has imploded. Here, we witness a phenomena; within the space of a single file, space has become too large to navigate. Titles, chapters, and formatting, are no longer readable in swaths of information overload. Naturally, this is due to the 1Dimensional nature of literature.

### 3.1 Historical evolution of the link
A link, is a powerful, undervalued piece of technology. In it's most primitive form, is simply a pointer to a piece of information. _[e.g. a footnote number, is a primitve form of a link, acting as plain-text, but with a purpose, other than providing information, rather, redirecting information.]_ The historical primitive of a link can be found throughout ancient archaelogical writings. 

The modern usage of links, have essentially, inlined the concept, to live within the text. This is a radical invention; words within literature are not technologized and clickable as explorable paths of information. This is, in essnece, close to [how the human brain processes information, within literature.](#)

## Files
Files are the smallest data fragment that we are used to working with, in our modern digitally-driven lives. However, it is only true for computers. The smallest bit of information for the human, isn't even literature, it's pre-linguistic.

So the file, is the smallest object of data, that a computer-and-human can both work with. A computer can write memory directly into bits. We can write letters and symbols too. But the file, is the data-model of portability. A shape. A thing you can store and rename. 

It emerges from the office days of early computing, when programmers needed a way to tether this immense power of computers to the real world. And thus, it explains why so many of these concepts, can be traced back to office cultures.

### Atomic files
Atomic files are also worth emphasizing as a data model, although they are more of a approach to structuring files. the concept of the atomic file is a emphasized model. in essence, the atomic file created interconnected system in which information exists as points in a graph that can be interconnected. this is how the internet web is structured, and it is also how the human mind functions, roughly speaking 

index files, or another form of file categorization. Index files essentially hold links to other files. To make deep directories navigable as single layer interfaces. essentially converting 2D (folder-tree) into 1D (link a, link b, etc.)

## Folders
with 20+ files, information implodes and organization data models become necessary to navigate information. this is usually where folders emerge as containers that collapse and abstract information. (e.g. a chapter in a book, or a collapsible sublist are all examples of the abstract data model (folders))

## Hyperautomation
This might sound crazy, but automating, the automation is actually a necessary evolution in a system. And, in programming, it's basically what we're doing all the time. Building systems that manage smaller systems for us, etc.

Essentially, using computers to build tools to help organize our work, automatically. Reducing input, but not agency.
