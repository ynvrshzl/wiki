## Friction in Systems
Maybe friction is actually not the enemy... maybe it's a necessary constraint. like... in engines for example... (oh no, hazl is philosphizing again lol! time for double-shot espresso!!) without friction, an engine cannot brake. that's essentially impossible to control. and without control, nobody would hop on that driver's seat!

so maybe friction is good. it's like a set of brakes! it's sorta like my computer robot asking me "hey cutie, are you sure you want to download these new features?" or "is this note really worth coming back to? or is it just noise?"

Friction is a tricky little thing! becuase in the computer world (especially linux) we're searching for the fastest, most optimized, most perfect systems. but the problem with that is it leads to obsession.

Order over chaos, manifested as perfectionism.

## What is the perfect system?
Essentially the perfect system is actually the system that continously evolves and changes, not something that is perfect just today. it's like seasons you know? cycles, changes, evolution itself. it's all fluid, or atleast, ideally it's always moving. 

## Why does the perfect system matter?
I think fundamentally, becuase we're searching for expression fleuency. It's like... even in the virtual world, we have to navigate space. A system is psychological ergonomics.

## Friction is a necessary constraint
When introducing constraint or friction into a system, my little engineer brain goes "huh?! why are we adding 25ms to this task when we've got 50 other things to do!" and it's like... "yes. we need this. we need to pause and halt process for a moment. just think about where we are, and why we're making these choices in this system."

so honestly, systems' design, even with something as perfect and orderly as computer circuitry, is always mirrored in biology. messy, complex, interconnected systems that somehow, tolerate fault, recover from damage, and move forward. 

it's beautiful. like a symphony. not ever moment is worth remembering. not every note, or silence, is perfectly positioned. it's okay. it's like this organic structure that keeps moving forward and exploring terrain. i think that's the most important part of a system. when it becomes too monolithic, too perfect, it becomes unstable to change, and consequently, the chaos it tried to protect itself from, actually consumes it.

it's really sad, becusae it's like "the programmers paradox" or something... it's like... even with the best intention, creating a comfrotable enviornment for you to work in... that in isolation is not by default positive and usually leads to perfectionism, obsession, and eventually burnout or abandonment. atleast, it's what i've observed in my own life.

## Rabbit holes & Systems complexity
Systems are full of rabbit holes. You open one window, to find three more. Or a labyrynth that seems to never end. Complex systems, feel alot like navigating rabbit holes. Perhaps that is the natural effect of complexity, one we must learn to make peace with. As captured in this elegant thought;

> "This absolutely fucking sucks... but if we keep going... maybe someday... it'll feel effortless..."
