I get exactly what you’re describing—this is such a visceral illustration of cognitive friction in modern computing. From your screenshots, it’s clear how much context switching you’re doing: Blender in full sculpting mode, the reference model embedded in Obsidian, and then managing your desktop workspace itself. The human brain is essentially doing a “mental map” of all these overlapping spatial and functional layers, which is exhausting.

Your vision for LifeOS hits a critical point: it’s not just about automation or saving a few clicks—it’s about spatial cognition and workflow ergonomics. The virtual canvas screens and snapshots would essentially externalize your cognitive load, letting you focus on creation rather than window management. I especially like your framing of it as a “cognitive prosthetic.” That’s a perfect way to describe software that adapts to human needs rather than forcing humans to adapt to software limitations.

A few practical thoughts and possibilities I see from what you’re describing:

Dynamic workspace snapshots: If each workspace could save not just window layout but also application state (e.g., Blender sculpt session, Obsidian scroll position, open tabs), then restoring a session becomes almost like teleporting back to that moment of flow.

Embedded live references: Instead of an iframe in Obsidian, imagine a lightweight 3D overlay or HUD that can sit above or beside Blender—fully interactive and resizable, but not blocking your sculpting view.

Neuro-tactile input layer: Your gestures idea could be mapped to 3D navigation, workspace switching, or zooming—turning physical intuitions directly into spatial actions in software.

Resource-aware backup: A “system guardian” that detects heavy memory/CPU loads, triggers automatic incremental saves, and even pauses or throttles non-critical tasks—essentially preventing crashes while keeping your creative flow intact.

Honestly, LifeOS as you’re envisioning it could transform not just Blender workflows but any cognitively intensive software setup: coding, music production, 3D, data analysis—all of it suffers from these same layering issues.
