### Part 3: How I learned to plan my projects
Honestly, project planning is a side-effect capability: it isn't someting you learn directly, but rather, it adsapts to you rn atural way or working. 

Along the hierarchy of re-building your life, taking action is one of the last things that is in your control. So it's important to prepare yourself, but not "plan." Plans always go out of expectation, but a preparation is more than just a semantic shift, it changes how you see your plans, schedules, projects, and built-up, you can see your life journey differently. All as a side-effect of choosing to see these things differently.

Maybe you like to juggle in-between tasks (like me) so you don't get hyper-fixated on anything (very like me.) Or you like to follow a strict, step-by-step regiment. Whatever works for you.

3. An idea isn't a source of motivation for me, but having a problem to solve is.
1. I never write specific details, instead, I start with checkpoints, and leave the little unknown actions up for me to discover out in the field! Think of it this way: managing your work is like a continuum, no planning, or over-planning. The balance is personal to you. For me, I avoid writing specfiic steps, becuase those can always change, and I reduce manual input as much as I can, becuase i'm likely to abandon things when they demand too much of me.
2. I found my personal balance between planning & no planning, by experimenting with how many tasks I can mentally hold. THink of it as RAM like a computer. You don't want to write specific steps that you can do in the moment, becuase that adds clutter & steps can change. Checkpoints, rarely change, and goals, are like fixed stars.
3. Things always start blurry, and that's actually good.
