here's something fascinating, as part of my effort, a series of studies, models, tools, research, and phenomenological recordings, of essentially figuring out how to navigate the modern Life as a human being in the 21st century, in the information age, the age of "excess access"

nearly all fields that study human life and systems, like cybernetics, and information theory, are used as resources and citations throughout this body of work. 

but without getting lost in the semantics of the work, I want to focus at a high level, one of the most interesting concepts; the phenomena of lack of instruments or tools, general ambiguity in virtual technologies, and how we human beings evolved to use tools, instruments, and models to solve problems in the natural world. 

we can think of it this way; virtual technologies directly interface with human psychology and cognition; our physical proprioception, ergonomics, or physical abilities, are secondary characteristics (at least this is my current hypothesis...)

technology itself transfers information through two primary channels, to streams of sensory input; audio and visual. the visual is the primary field, and it is connected to focus; visuals are highly engaging for the human nervous system as sensory stimuli. audio is surprisingly less commonly used, but it is just as engaging, as according to anthropological/archaeological recordings, or human ancestors often relied on sound, as a primary century stream for survival, especially when we could no longer rely on our sight (visual.)

the fact that audio is very rarely used in technology today, is a sign that we are in a very early age of this new kind of a sensory environment... I believe it's also important to treat technology as a sensory environment, because that shifts responsibility from "the human should have discipline with technology" to "graphical user interfaces, design, and virtual experiences, are non-neutral; they interfaced directly with human sensory processing."

before getting lost any deeper in the theory, I want to focus on two branching concepts; 
1. we don't have the physical equivalent of a body in virtual space, and yet we experience virtual space as a physical phenomena (...)
2. the fact that there are no tools or instruments (at least according to physical models) in virtual spaces, suggests that the field needs to patch up with human biological needs, before software becomes fully autonomous beyond our capacity to understand it (LLMs and AI-generated code systems for example.)

I could imagine one of the foundational tools for virtual space today, is something I use regularly and honestly depend on; an artificial / synthetic / virtual memory system. essentially a file system in a computer. the more specialized a file system can become, the more it resembles human cognitive abilities, to remember and process and store and more importantly navigate information, but as a synthetic system. 

... speaking of navigation, another core tool would be the ability to filter, query, and use programming logic, to work with data and navigate the systems, to make sense of otherwise non-final, non-linear information spaces. this is probably what the nervous system is doing at a high level, with the natural world presenting non finite, nonlinear sensory streams of parallel information... in fact we studied this deeply in a separate field, but it is integral here as well.
